# Vision

## Purpose
Define why this SaaS starter exists, what problem it solves, and what strategic path it should follow.

## Core thesis
Most technical founders lose 2 to 8 weeks rebuilding the same SaaS foundation: auth, billing, onboarding, dashboard structure, settings, database plumbing, and environment setup. Existing starters often fail on one of two extremes:
- too shallow to accelerate a serious launch,
- too abstract or over-engineered to customize quickly.

This product should win by offering a starter that is production-ready, commercially credible, simple to adapt, and clearly worth a premium one-time purchase.

## Product vision
Build a premium AI-native Next.js SaaS starter for solo founders and small technical teams who want to launch a real B2B SaaS fast without inheriting a confusing internal framework.

## Strategic objective
Create a sellable asset that saves buyers meaningful time, reduces setup risk, shortens time-to-revenue, and supports a price floor of 149 USD without feeling cheap.

## Target outcome for the buyer
Within 1 to 7 days, a buyer should be able to:
- run the app locally,
- understand the codebase structure,
- customize branding and key flows,
- connect billing and database,
- ship a first usable SaaS version.

## Positioning statement
An AI-native, monetization-ready Next.js SaaS starter built for launch speed, clarity, and customization — not for architectural vanity.

## What this product is
- a sellable foundation for launching B2B or prosumer SaaS products,
- a clear codebase with strong defaults,
- a monetization-ready base with essential product flows,
- a system optimized for speed of understanding and customization,
- a premium product positioned above cheap boilerplates.

## What this product is not
- not a generic template for every possible app category,
- not an enterprise framework,
- not a playground of every modern pattern,
- not a code museum designed to impress senior architects,
- not a low-ticket commodity boilerplate.

## Strategic constraints
- Simplicity must beat sophistication when trade-offs are close.
- Commercial value must beat technical cleverness.
- The starter must feel premium immediately through UI, docs, and onboarding.
- Every feature added must strengthen perceived buyer value.
- The product must stay understandable to a competent Next.js developer without extra doctrine.
- The pricing floor is 149 USD.

## Governing standards
- Structural and folder decisions default to `nextjs-saas-structure`.
- Buyer-fit and abstraction judgments default to `starter-buyer-fit`.

## Key hypotheses
1. Buyers will pay 149 USD or more for a starter that removes monetization and setup friction.
2. Buyers care more about customization speed than architectural sophistication.
3. Clear documentation materially increases conversion and reduces refund and support risk.
4. AI-native positioning can differentiate the starter if it does not add buyer-facing complexity.

## Risks
- Over-scoping before real buyer validation.
- Building for developers in general instead of one clear ICP.
- Excessive abstractions that slow customization.
- Weak positioning causing the product to look like another generic starter.
- Letting AI-native ambition bloat V1 beyond what supports a 149+ offer.

## Open questions
- Which AI-native capabilities create immediate value without bloating V1?
- What is the best Starter vs Pro split while keeping 149 USD compelling?
- What license/update policy best supports premium perception without support overload?

## Success criteria
- Buyers understand the offer in under 30 seconds.
- Buyers can follow setup docs without live help.
- The codebase feels obvious to a competent Next.js developer.
- The starter saves enough time to justify a premium one-time price.
- The product feels credible at 149 USD and naturally upsells to higher tiers.
